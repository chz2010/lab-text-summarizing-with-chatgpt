# One-Page Report: Text Summarization Findings

In this lab, I tested three different summarization versions using longer AI-related texts instead of short ecommerce reviews. Overall, GPT performed well at producing concise summaries and captured the central idea of each passage. The results showed that the model is flexible and can summarize academic, historical, and opinion-based content even when the prompt is not perfectly written. At the same time, the lab also showed that prompt wording affects what kind of summary the model produces and how much nuance it preserves.

In the first version, I used a passage based on *Attention Is All You Need*. GPT summarized it clearly by focusing on the main contribution of the Transformer model, its advantage over recurrent and convolutional architectures, and its strong translation performance. This variation worked well because the source text had one central idea, so the model could compress it effectively without losing the main message. The output was accurate and concise, and I did not notice any hallucination in this version.

In the second version, I used a broader historical overview of artificial intelligence. After rerunning it, GPT produced a solid summary that described AI’s evolution from the 1940s to the present, its wide range of disciplines and applications, and its impact on society. This result worked better than before and stayed aligned with the passage. The only weakness was in the prompt itself, which still referred to *Attention Is All You Need* even though the source text was about AI history in general. Even with that mismatch, the model did not hallucinate and still returned a relevant summary, which suggests that GPT can often recover from imperfect instructions when the text is clear.

In the third version, I used a text discussing how quickly AI capabilities have improved in recent years, especially in terms of coherence and usefulness in scientific work. GPT produced a short summary emphasizing the rapid improvement of AI systems and the surprise of experts at that progress. This version worked well overall, but it also showed one limitation of summarization: the final answer was correct, yet it left out some nuance from the original passage. For example, the source text also discussed transparency, customization, and the comparison between older neural networks and newer foundation models. These details were not false or hallucinated in the summary; they were simply omitted because the response had to stay brief.

Looking across all three versions, I found that GPT was generally reliable and did not produce clear hallucinations. The weaker points were not invented facts, but prompt mismatch and loss of nuance. Version 2 still had the least precise instruction because the prompt named the wrong thesis, while Version 3 was accurate but more selective than detailed. From this exercise, I learned three main lessons. First, GPT can summarize many kinds of text well, even outside the original product-review setting. Second, prompts should be updated carefully so that the instructions match the actual document being summarized. Third, a summary can be accurate without being complete, so it is important to decide whether the goal is brevity, detail, or topic-specific focus. Overall, this lab helped me understand that effective summarization depends not only on the model, but also on how clearly the task is framed.
